# AGENTS.md

A primer for AI agents and new contributors working on the Honch codebase.
Read this first. It explains *what we're building and why* — not how it's
implemented. For implementation details, look at the code.

---

## What Honch is

Honch is a product analytics platform for connected consumer hardware.

The closest existing reference point is PostHog, but for physical devices
instead of web apps. Customers build cameras, e-bikes, smart home
devices, fitness wearables, pet trackers, premium appliances — anything
that ships, runs firmware, and connects to the internet. Honch tells
them how those products are actually used in the field.

The fundamental problem we solve: hardware product managers have no idea
what's happening with their devices once they ship. They can guess based
on returns and support tickets and the occasional survey. They can't
answer "what percentage of X3 users have ever used HDR mode?" — a
question their web counterparts answer in five seconds with Mixpanel.
We close that gap.

## The positioning

"Product analytics for the hardware you ship."

Hardware teams currently solve this problem in one of two ways:

1. They build their own pipeline (Snowflake, custom dashboards, a data
   engineer or two). Works at scale, expensive, slow to iterate.
2. They adapt web analytics tools that weren't designed for hardware.
   Mixpanel, Amplitude, PostHog all have hardware customers but none of
   them speak the hardware vocabulary or handle the hardware constraints.

We're the third option: a product-analytics platform built for the
specific shape of hardware data.

## Who buys this

Our ICP is consumer hardware companies in the $200+ retail price band
with global distribution, software-driven products, and product teams
that don't have an embedded data team. Examples in our reference set:
Insta360, Eight Sleep, Fi (pet GPS), e-bike brands, premium audio
companies, fitness wearable startups, smart home hardware.

What we're explicitly not building for: industrial IoT, automotive
telematics, agricultural sensors, fleet management for trucks. Those
markets exist and are large but they have different vocabulary,
different buyers, and different regulatory loads. We pick consumer
hardware on purpose.

## The competitive landscape

The space we're entering has incumbents in adjacent slots:

- **Memfault** — embedded observability with a Product Analytics add-on.
  Engineer-first, enterprise-priced, observability-led. Bose, Logitech,
  Panasonic, Lyft are their customers. Our most direct competitor.
- **PostHog / Mixpanel / Amplitude** — generalist product analytics.
  Web-first, occasionally adapted by hardware companies. Don't speak
  hardware language.
- **Splunk / Datadog** — observability platforms, not analytics.
  Different buyer, different shape.
- **In-house Snowflake stacks** — what most hardware companies have
  today. Expensive to maintain, slow to iterate.

Our wedge against Memfault: we're PM-native, not engineer-native.
Their dashboards are designed for firmware engineers reading crash
traces. Ours are designed for product managers running roadmap
meetings. Funnels, retention, cohorts, dashboards — best-in-class
product analytics with hardware-specific primitives.

## What we believe (and you should too if you're working here)

Some opinions we hold strongly. If a design decision conflicts with
these, push back.

**Hardware data is fundamentally different from web data.** It arrives
out of order, sometimes days late. It's generated by devices that
sometimes can't reach the internet. It's tied to physical objects that
have firmware versions, hardware revisions, owners, sessions, batteries,
radios. Treat it like web data and the model breaks within a quarter.

**Sessions in hardware are nothing like sessions on the web.** Web
sessions are 30-minute timeout buckets. Hardware sessions are
*developer-declared spans of meaningful activity* — a recording, a
workout, a charge cycle, a flight. They have names. They have
durations. They group events that meaningfully belong together. This
distinction is one of our most important product primitives.

**The SDK shape matters more than the cloud shape.** A great cloud
analytics platform with a bad SDK is unusable. The SDK is what
customers integrate, debug, and live with for years. Optimize SDK
ergonomics, footprint, and reliability above almost everything else.

**Customers can't tolerate wire format churn.** Once their firmware
ships, the wire format is frozen for that fleet. Design with the
assumption that you can never break it again. Add fields, don't
rename them. Tolerate missing properties. Default conservatively.

**A PM should be able to use this product without understanding our
infrastructure.** If a question we answer requires understanding
ClickHouse vs Postgres, or what "denormalized" means, we've failed at
abstraction. The customer's mental model is: "I have devices, my users
do things on them, I want to know what they do." Every UI surface
should respect that.

## Core entities and what they mean

These are the nouns in the product. Get them right and the rest of the
system follows.

### Project

A project represents one product line at one customer. Insta360 has
one project for cameras (covering X3, X4, X5 and future SKUs). They
might have a separate project for drones. All cross-cutting analytics
happen within a project. Cross-project queries are rare and usually
indicate a modeling mistake.

A project belongs to an organization. An organization is the billing
and admin boundary; a project is the analytics boundary.

### Event

The atomic unit. Every interaction a device or companion app reports
becomes one event. Events have a name (e.g. `hdr_burst_used`,
`$device_boot`), a time when they happened on the device, a distinct_id
(who or what generated it), and a properties bag.

Two kinds of events exist:
- **Custom events** — defined by the customer's firmware code.
  `button_pressed`, `recording_started`, `firmware_update_failed`.
- **Lifecycle events** — defined by us, prefixed with `$`. Emitted
  automatically by the SDK. `$device_boot`, `$session_start`,
  `$identify`, `$battery_low`, etc.

Events are immutable. Once captured, never edited. Always append-only.

### Device

A physical thing in the world running our SDK. Every device has a
stable identifier that persists across reboots and firmware updates
but resets on factory reset. One person can own many devices. One
device might be transferred between people over its lifetime (rentals,
family-shared cameras, returned units).

### Device model

A SKU. The product line description: "Insta360 X3 Pro" or "Orbit Mini."
Customers manage device models (mostly via the dashboard, sometimes
auto-created when a new model arrives in events). Device models are
small (5-20 per project) and slow-changing.

The relationship is many-to-one: many devices map to one model. The
model holds shared metadata: display name, description, deprecation
status, retail price, category, anything else the customer wants to
attach.

### Person

An end-user. A human. The customer's customer. Persons are merged
across distinct_ids over time — anonymous events from a device get
attributed to a person record initially keyed by the device, and when
the customer's app calls `identify()` with a real user identifier
(email, customer ID), the anonymous person merges into the identified
one.

A person can have multiple distinct_ids (the device-based one from
when they were anonymous, plus the real user_id after identification).
A person can be linked to multiple devices.

### Session

A developer-declared span of related activity. NOT a browser-style
timeout bucket. The customer's firmware calls `session_start("recording")`
when REC is pressed and `session_end()` when STOP is pressed. Every
event in between gets stamped with that session's ID and name.

Sessions are how we answer "what features get used during a recording?"
or "what's the average workout duration?" They're one of the most
important primitives we offer that web analytics tools don't have a
clean equivalent for.

Each session has an ID, a name (the string the developer passed, e.g.
"recording"), a start time, an end time, a duration, and the events
that belong to it.

### Distinct ID

A string that identifies *who or what* generated an event. Before
identification, it's the device's identifier. After identification,
it's whatever the customer's app passes to `identify()` (email,
customer ID, internal user ID, etc.).

A person can have multiple distinct_ids; they get linked through the
identify flow. A device has exactly one device identifier but the
events from that device may carry different distinct_ids over time as
users change.

### Property

A key-value attribute on an event, person, or device. Custom properties
are defined by the customer's firmware (`{"shot_count": 12, "mode":
"hdr"}`). Auto-stamped properties are added by the SDK on every event
(device ID, model, firmware version, etc., all prefixed with `$`).

### Lexicon

The customer's view of their own taxonomy. Every event name and
property key the customer has ever sent. Customers edit display names
and descriptions to make their data readable to non-technical
teammates. The Lexicon is also the source of truth for the dropdowns
in other parts of the dashboard (funnel builders, filter pickers,
breakdown selectors).

## The SDK landscape

We don't have one SDK. We have three categories of SDK, all
implementing the same wire contract.

### Device SDK

Runs on customer hardware. Talks directly to our cloud over the
customer's connectivity (Wi-Fi, Ethernet, future cellular).

Platforms include ESP-IDF, Zephyr, Arduino, bare-metal C for STM32
and nRF, embedded Linux, Rust embedded, MicroPython.

### App SDK

Runs on companion mobile apps (iOS, Android, React Native, Flutter).
Has two modes:

- **Standalone mode:** the app sends events directly to our cloud
  about user behavior in the app. Same shape as a normal mobile
  analytics SDK.
- **Relay mode:** the paired hardware device has events queued
  locally. When the user opens the app and the device is in range,
  the app drains the device's queue and forwards those events to our
  cloud, tagged as relayed.

The relay model is critical for products that don't have direct
internet (BLE-only earbuds, Zigbee/Thread devices, anything that only
phones home via the user's app).

### Module Host SDK

A thin wrapper for customers using our cellular module (a future
hardware product). The customer's firmware writes events to the
module over a simple link; the module handles all the cloud
transport, queuing, and connectivity. The customer's firmware barely
knows the cloud exists.

## The relay model

Some hardware can't reach the cloud directly. Earbuds with no Wi-Fi.
Zigbee/Thread sensors. Low-power devices that only sync when their
companion app is open. For those, we relay through the customer's
mobile app.

Our principle: **we're a payload, not a protocol.** We don't own the
channel between the device and the phone — the customer's app and
firmware already have a relationship for their own product features.
We just give them an opaque byte blob to transport and an SDK function
to ingest it on the other side.

The relay tag on events tells us which path they took, so we can
distinguish direct-from-device events from relayed ones in the dashboard.

## Time-skew is real

Events generated months ago can arrive today. A camera that was offline
for six weeks, a device whose companion app was uninstalled and
reinstalled, a BLE-relayed event that sat in the firmware queue until
the user paired again — all normal.

Every event has two timestamps: the one set on the device when the
event happened (used for analytics), and the one set in the cloud when
the event was received (used for ingest debugging). These can differ by
hours, days, or months. Don't write code that assumes they're close.

## The dashboard

The customer-facing UI. Four conceptual groups in the navigation:

- **Home** — the landing page when a customer clicks into a project
- **Analyze** — Insights, Funnels, Retention, Dashboards. PM-facing
  analytics surfaces.
- **Data** — Events, Devices, Persons, Lexicon. Engineer/CX-facing
  entity browsing.
- **Monitor** — Live event stream, Alerts. Real-time and operational.

Plus Settings at the bottom.

### Page conventions

- Tabs use **primary underline** style (2px underline on active, no
  underline on inactive)
- IDs are always rendered in monospace
- Time is always relative ("2 hours ago") with the absolute timestamp
  on hover
- Lists are dense, optimized for scanning
- Detail panes do the work of showing depth — list rows stay minimal
- Every entity reference is a link (device -> device detail, person ->
  person detail, session -> events filtered by session)
- Filter state lives in URL query params so links are shareable

### What the dashboard does NOT do

- It doesn't replace the customer's own data warehouse. We're product
  analytics, not a data lake.
- It doesn't try to host the customer's internal BI. Customers with
  Tableau or Looker keep them.
- It doesn't manage devices (no OTA updates, no remote control, no
  configuration push). We're read-only on the device side.
- It doesn't surface PII beyond what's necessary. Distinct IDs and
  properties only. Email, phone, address etc. should be opaque hashes
  if customers pass them at all.

## Things we're not building (and why)

To save future you from re-litigating these decisions:

**Session replay.** The web-analytics pattern of recording every user
interaction doesn't transfer to hardware. Devices don't have
screen-grabbable UIs. Don't propose it.

**A/B testing / feature flags.** Different product. Important market,
not ours. Customers can A/B test by sharding firmware versions and
filtering analytics on firmware version.

**OTA / firmware update management.** Memfault's lane, not ours. Sales
prospects will ask. The answer is "use Memfault for firmware updates;
use us for what they don't do well."

**Crash dumps and core file analysis.** Same answer. Memfault's lane.

**Live device control / remote management.** Not analytics. Different
product, different buyer.

**Custom SQL access.** Tempting because it's "an escape hatch." Resist.
It pushes us toward being a generic analytics tool and away from
PM-first workflows.

**An on-device feature flag service.** Conflates product analytics
with experimentation infrastructure.

**A separate Sessions page in the sidebar.** Sessions are a dimension
we slice by, not an entity worth browsing. They appear as filters in
events, as sections in device detail, and as a "scope to session"
toggle in funnels. Resist building a top-level Sessions browser.

## Some defensible opinions about hardware product analytics

These show up in customer conversations and product decisions.

**Funnels segmented by firmware version are the killer feature.** Every
customer demo should end with "look, we can show conversion broken down
by firmware 3.4 vs 3.5." This is the moment that closes deals. Web
analytics tools can't do this naturally.

**Retention by hardware revision is a real question.** When a
manufacturer changes a sensor between rev-A and rev-B, the PM wants to
know if usage patterns changed. Most analytics tools can't even
represent the question.

**"Active device" is more useful than "active user."** A consumer
hardware company often doesn't know who their users are (anonymous
device events with no companion app). Device-as-MAU is the metric they
use internally; we should respect that.

**Hardware sessions enable a class of insights web tools can't
deliver.** "Average HDR bursts per recording session, segmented by
firmware," "median session duration on X4 vs X3," "session completion
rate." These exist only because customers explicitly declare sessions
around meaningful product activity.

**Time-to-first-use is the most important onboarding metric.** From
unboxing to first meaningful event is the funnel hardware PMs care
about most. Build for it.

## When in doubt

If you're an AI agent working on this codebase and a decision isn't
covered above:

1. The hardware-first framing wins over the web-first instinct. When
   the obvious "this is how Mixpanel does it" answer applies, double-
   check whether hardware breaks the assumption.
2. The SDK is a contract. Don't change the wire format without
   explicit human approval.
3. If the thing you're about to build feels like a feature Memfault
   already has, stop and reconsider positioning before continuing.
4. Customers are PMs and firmware engineers, in that order of priority
   for UX decisions. PMs use the dashboard daily. Firmware engineers
   integrate once and live with it for years. Both matter; neither
   dominates.
